Project Overview

Vision Statement

Project Vision

Music has always brought people together, but finding a space where musicians and enthusiasts can share their perspectives, discover new ideas, and feel genuinely heard is harder than it should be. DISKussMe! exists to change that. Rather than relying on large general-purpose social media platforms where music content can get buried or lost, DISKussMe! focuses entirely on helping users discover and connect through their shared interest in music.
DISKussMe! is a community forum built specifically for music lovers to share their thoughts, experiences, and knowledge about the music that moves them. Whether someone is a seasoned guitarist, a casual listener with strong opinions on genres, or a producer exploring new techniques, DISKussMe! gives them a place to put their voice into the world and connect with others who care just as deeply.
DISKussMe! makes it easy for users to create posts about artists, genres, instruments, performances, albums, or other music topics that matter to them. Users can browse posts, search for content that matches their interests, and explore the profiles of other users to better understand their musical background and perspective. By making it simple to view, create, and revisit music-related content, DISKussMe! aims to encourage users to engage more deeply with music and feel more connected to a community of people with similar interests.
DISKussMe! also supports self-expression by allowing users to personalize their profiles with information about their musical tastes, interests, and instruments they play. They are also able to manage their own content by editing or deleting posts. This helps users present who they are musically, keep their posts relevant and discover others with similar interests.
DISKussMe! will be considered successful when it becomes the go-to space for music-focused discussion. Success will be measured by attracting 500 registered users within the first three months of release, achieving a user retention rate of at least 50% from Month 1 to Month 3, and maintaining an average of 20 or more new posts per week sustained over that same period.

Intended Users

Target Audience

DISKussMe! is a music-focused app that empowers music lovers, from musicians to casual listeners, who want to share their perspectives, discover new ideas, and connect with others who share their passion for music.

02

Major Features

  • Users can log in and sign out.

    All users currently present in the database can use their credentials to log in to the system and use the application.

  • Users can create posts.

    Users can create posts with at minimum a title. They also have the option of adding a body and descriptive tags to provide more detail and make their posts easier for others to understand and discover.

  • Users can search and filter posts.

    In the post feed, users can view posts from all other users and use several filtering options to find relevant content more easily. Users can search within post content, search for specific users using the @ symbol, filter posts by descriptive tags, and sort posts by most recent or least recent.

  • Users can delete and edit their own posts when viewing them.

    If a user is unhappy with their post, they can open it to view it in more detail and then edit the title, body, and tags, or delete the post entirely.

  • Users can edit their own profile.

    Each user has their own profile that they can personalize by updating their name, description, tags, and profile photo. Users can also view other users' profiles to learn more about them by opening their posts and selecting their usernames.

Development Postmortem

Change

What Changed?

After our retrospective in Iteration 2 and a review of the SOLID violation identified by another group, we decided to focus on adding exception handling to the UI while also addressing the OCP violation. We also started to enforce the team splits less as we took ownership of features more than separate layers.

Outcome

Was the Improvement Successful?

We implemented a Fragment Factory to tackle the OCP violation, which also helped us to shorten DiskussMe­MainActivity. Although we initially thought this would address the OCP violation, we realized during this reflection that it merely moved the violation to another class, since the factory class must be modified if a new Fragment were added.
We also added try-catches in the UI for exceptions thrown by the logic layer. This made the app safer from crashing and ensured it would never fail silently. If a critical error occurs, it pops the fragment back stack to the most recent stable fragment and notifies the user of what happened. The specific issue has also been added to the Log to better fix errors.
This shift in the teams allowed us to more quickly add features and work through entire user flows individually.

Went Well

What Went Well

We worked very well as a team throughout the project. We consistently followed common agile development practices, ensuring that every merge request was reviewed by at least two team members before being merged. Our branching strategy was followed closely, and everyone maintained their GitLab issues so that progress and responsibilities were always clear.
We also communicated regularly. At minimum, we held one meeting each week to update one another, discuss challenges, and plan the remainder of the iteration. This helped us stay aligned and prevented work from falling behind.
Another strength was how we divided work across the architectural layers of the project. Team members were able to take ownership of specific areas, which created clear sources of knowledge when others needed help or had to work in those parts of the codebase. This made collaboration easier and reduced confusion.
In addition, we maintained a very positive, supportive, and open team culture. Everyone was transparent about their time constraints and comfortable asking for help when needed. As the project progressed, we built more trust in one another and improved our planning and scheduling processes, making it easier to track progress and requirements.
Lastly, we consistently upheld strong programming standards and respectfully held each other accountable. We gave frequent constructive feedback during reviews, which not only improved the quality of the project but also helped all of us grow as developers.

Went Wrong

What Went Wrong

Early on, we struggled with scheduling and planning, which made the Iteration 2 submission a major time crunch. This created a lot of stress for everyone and showed us that we needed stronger planning and earlier coordination throughout each iteration.
We also ran into technical issues with the project structure. The project had accidentally been set up inside an extra folder, which caused several hours of frustration when we needed to work with the file structure to implement database seeding.
Lastly, our Iteration 1 UI relied entirely on activities. As the app grew, this approach became limiting and made navigation difficult to implement. We made the difficult decision to refactor the UI to use fragments instead. Although the refactor was time-consuming and frustrating, it was ultimately worth it because it gave us a more flexible and maintainable structure for the rest of the project.

Tech Debt

Outstanding Bugs or Technical Debt

A piece of technical debt that did not get resolved is the ability to create an account on the DiskussMe platform. In iterations 2 & 3, we created the ability for users to login and logout of the application, better separating the current user from others. However, if a new user wanted to join the platform, they have no way to register an account and will simply be met with a login page they can’t complete.
The technical debt is deliberate and prudent. The ability to add new users is out of the scope of our current project goals. Specifically, this is an implementation detail we will “deal with later” prior to an official public release.
Given one more iteration, to implement new user registration, we would need the following in each layer.
Presentation: A button located on the login page linked to a new fragment that allows the user to create a unique username and password. Other profile features such as a picture, tags and description would be available to edit after account creation in the profile tab.
Logic: A set of functions to ensure the user does not already exist. That is, the username is unique and the password meets certain expectations (longer then 5 characters, contains a number, etc.)
Persistence: The ability to store a new user.

Challenges

Unexpected Technical Challenges

An unexpected technical challenge arose from not understanding the limitations and behavior of Android Studio. During iteration 1, while the developers were first learning Android Studio, every UI page was created as an activity. This caused issues in iteration 2, when attempting to implement a navigation bar. Android Studio’s built-in navigation library is designed to be used with fragments rather than activates since they are handled differently on the backstack. This meant, every UI file needed to be refactored from an activity into a fragment before anymore work could take place in the presentation layer. Although this technical debt was a lot of work, the refactoring lead to cleaner code that better adhered to SOLID principles.
Another unexpected technical challenge came from the way the project was initially set up. The entire application had accidentally been placed inside an extra outer folder, which caused issues with how Android Studio resolved and fetched files. This became a problem when we wanted to use Android’s assets folder for our seed data, since the incorrect file structure prevented it from working properly. To fix it, we had to remove the unnecessary folder and reorganize the project structure. Although the change sounded simple, it ended up affecting nearly 200 files because so many file paths had to be updated. It also unexpectedly introduced a GitLab pipeline issue that we did not understand since our project does not use pipelines. While frustrating and time-consuming, solving this problem gave us a much cleaner project structure and prevented even larger issues later in development.

Evolution

Evolution from Original Vision

In iteration 0, our initial vision of the project was very large. We had planned to add many features to the app, and planned to eventually add features like comments, an admin dashboard, and a dark mode setting. We also discussed the possibility of adding playable music files but decided it was too difficult. When we started programming in iteration 1, the reality of our time constraints set in, and we realized that adding most of our features planned for iteration 1 was nearly impossible. We ended up scaling down the goals of the project. By the end of iteration 3, only about half of the features planned in iteration 0 were added to the app.

Lessons

Lessons Learned About Teamwork and Large Projects

At the beginning of the project, we split roles by architecture layer, two people for persistence, two for logic and two for presentation with a plan to change roles after each iteration. Shortly, we realized it was more effective for team members to specialize in a single layer of the project rather than having a weaker general understanding of each layer. This encouraged proper modularity and clear separation between layers and classes, as developers working on different layers understood the purpose of the code without needing to know its implementation. Lastly, we implemented a validation system; each merge request needed to be reviewed statically and run on the local machine of two other developers before it could be merged in.
